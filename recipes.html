* Café Americano

The Americano was born in Italy during World War II. American soldiers found traditional Italian espresso way too strong, so they diluted it with hot water to mimic the drip coffee they had back home.

The traditional americano is straightforward yet distinctive. To get it right, a one-to-two ratio of espresso to water is ideal, resulting in a deliciously full-bodied coffee that’s not too strong to be savored. First, prepare about 6 oz (180 ml) of water slightly less than boiling, then pour it in the cup. Afterward, make a fresh double espresso shot and then add it to the hot water. Doing this helps maintain the creamy layer of crema, adding to the aroma and taste.

* Café Latte

"Latte" simply means milk in Italian. A classic latte is the most milk-heavy espresso drink designed to be creamy, mellow, and lightly sweet.

Making a latte is about finding balance between espresso, which is strong in flavor, and milk, which adds creaminess and mellowness to the beverage. Traditionally, the ratio used is one part espresso to three parts steamed milk, topped with an airy thin film of micro-foam. Begin by making a double shot of espresso and pouring it into a high glass or wide mug. Next, heat up 6-8 oz of milk, ensuring that the milk looks shiny and creamy as opposed to frothy. Gently pour the heated milk into the middle of the espresso, leaving out the top foam layer until the cup is filled almost to the top.

* Caffè Cappuccino

If you love a rich, texture-forward coffee with a dense cushion of foam, this is your drink. It’s a traditional Italian masterpiece defined by its perfectly equal layers of espresso, milk, and thick micro-foam.

Cappuccinos consist of equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and thick milk foam, which make it robust, light, and textured. First, make a double espresso shot in a small 6 to 8 oz coffee cup. Second, steam cold milk and hold the steam wand just above the surface for a few seconds to incorporate air into the milk and create the foamy layer. The milk should be heated to a temperature of 150 degrees Fahrenheit, at which point you can tap and swish the contents of the jug to ensure a well-blended foam. Then, pour it steadily into the middle of your espresso and lower the jug slightly above the level of your beverage to produce a foam cap on top.
